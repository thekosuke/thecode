MAGAZINE CONCEPT | APRIL 2019

KYBRD

A magazine concept in response to the consequences we face when new technologies are introduced to us. Despite its capacity to bring hope and create new opportunities for mankind, it could also lead to unexpected human behaviors that reveal an unfortunate side of us. KYBRD is a magazine that aims to educate the younger audience with the ethical use of technology.

CONTEXT

An individual magazine design project for a graphic design course at Simon Fraser University.

TYPE

Branding, Art Direction, Visual Design

CREDITS

Illustrations taken from Giovanna Giuliano

BRAND CONCEPT

Digital Etiquette

Editorial Mandate

An advocate for the righteous use of technology, KYBRD is committed to publishing unbiased content for those who are willing to be informed about the digital offerings they use throughout their daily lives.

Each edition shares insight on current events surrounding the digital culture while promoting ethical interactions within the online community, in order to raise awareness of the repercussions surrounding the use of advanced technology through a language that is comprehensible to the younger audience.

The magazine is distributed through academic institutions, community centers, and other facilities where young teenagers are likely to be exposed to print materials.

Target Readership

The target reader is a young student (age 12 - 18) who is curious about the implications of technology in today’s context and aspires to learn about the intersection of people, culture and technology. The reader is up to date about current news related to the tech culture.

BRAND IDENTITY

Cultural Reference

Art Direction

The art direction was inspired by the pop culture aesthetic from the 80’s and 90’s, in which vibrant colors and modern typography were often applied to a wide range of designs such as casette packaging. The choice of modern typography align with the brand’s objective to express the hope that technology brings through the use of vibrant colors.

Considering that the magazine’s primary target demographic is Gen Z, who are not familiar with this aesthetic like the Millennials are, the design will be adapted to the modern context with cultural reference to the period in which introduced the joy of technology with the contribution of products like iMac G3, Game Boy Color, Playstation and many more.

Typeface

When designing the logotype, I began the process by selecting a typeface that best represents KYBRD as a brand, while reflecting the energy of the young readers. With a playful, yet precise form, GT Walsheim contains unique characteristics that distinguish it from conventional sans serifs. With eight different weights and a condensed version, the typeface offers flexibility to explore various ways to achieve hierarchy.

The use of uppercase for the logotype was an aestheic decision since despite the legibility, the lowercase ‘k’, ‘b’ and ‘d’ created undesirable spaces within the overall form, whereas the uppercase letters become a geometrical block that act as a clean visual element within the magazine cover.

The unique characteritic of the letter ‘Y’ was the driving factor when selecting the typeface as the angled ‘Y’ became an element of recognition with the aspiration to deliver a memorable logo. The negative spaces created between ‘K’ and ‘B’ introduced an interesting relationship among the letters. The space between the last three letters were tightened to achieve an asymmetrical balance, as a symmetrical form can be too settling for the eye. The intent was to bring attention to the first two letters with the use of negative space.


Final Logo

The four colors were added to bring a playful tone to the bold typeface, which was complimented by the addition of four stripes that gave a distinguishable characteristic to the overall form. The combination of warm and cool colors were chosen due to the contrast it created with each other, as well as the monochromatic color scheme. This was initially inspired by Paul Rand’s IBM logo, in which the 13 stripes became an iconic representation of technology that IBM stood for.

MAGAZINE DESIGN

Reflecting the Youth

Cover Design

The cover was designed to capture the essence of youth to grab the target demographic’s attention with the use of playful illustrations, vibrant colors, and bold typography. This was executed by using illustrated emojis that can also be found in the feature article, as it helps establish a consistent theme across the issue. I chose to exclude other cover lines and hero the feature story in order to reflect the bold and playful brand identity.


The monochrome logotype was used since the color version was fighting with the colorful illustration for attention. The logotype will be alternated with the color version depending on that issue’s cover design since the use of illustration is specific to this feature story. The idea was to have a logotype that could adapt to all types of graphics in order to portray a playful brand consistently through each issue.

Feature Story

To align with the topic of the story, the editorial layout design mimics the Facebook brand with their brand color and illustrations that is reminiscent of the one’s used on their platform. With an target audience of age 13 to 19, a graphic-heavy layout with a larger body copy was necessary to keep readers engaged. The tone of the color remained consistent with the cover design while portraying the Facebook brand with the extended use of blue.

Spread Layout

The second spread delivers an unconventional way of reading as it visually represents a timeline with a path that guide readers through the content. Using bold numbers while breaking the grid with a curved path allowed for a dynamic layout that align with the brand concept.

Table of Contents

The Table of Contents was designed first with the readers in mind. The intention was to create a layout with a clear hierarchy that communicated the content within the entire magazine.

Subscription Card

The campaign was created with the intent to increase yearly subscribers by emphasizing the price of the special offer. With more subscribers for the print edition, the magazine will be able to increase revenue in advertisements. This was done by applying color and bolding the price of the offer to grab the readers’ attention. With the call to action being the largest text, the perks of subscribing was placed underneath to allow readers to understand the value in a quick glance.
