Background

Remotion is a virtual office that makes room for casual conversations with coworking and shared music. It is a macOS app that lets you pin your teammates to the dock to have background awareness of what people are up to.

I was brought in as a product designer in the Core Experience team to make focused improvements to the core app. The team consisted of 5 engineers and myself, with the goal to increase retention and word of mouth sharing.

Roadmap

The Core Experience Team's roadmap was driven by the most critical problems that led to user churn. Thus our goal was to hit the "Beta Bar" — a lower quality bar that we’re willing to accept as we’re in a period of rapid iteration. Even in this state, we need to provide an experience that’s good enough where a user likes our product enough to want to share it with others.

We focused on solving problems within the core workflows before investing in brand new features that drive new user value (which would be GA).

Coworking with Music

The purpose of Coworking with Music is to foster a low stress atmosphere in which teams are able to work alongside each other in a common room. A good music experience in coworking rooms should “just work”, though people were confused about some of the UI and its behaviors when they were coworking in a Remotion Room.

Broadly speaking, the main design objectives were to bring more clarity and consistency in regards to what is happening in the room, educate users how rooms work, and to curate a shared experience for teams to cowork seamlessly. These problems were addressed holistically by identifying opportunities to reach our goals in different surfaces. They were prioritized based on the amount of customer feedback and the severity of the issue.

Music Ducking

In Remotion, Rooms are where teams come together to regularly work side-by-side with music playing in the background. When someone starts talking in a Room, Remotion automatically lowers the music volume so people can hear each other talk — we referred to this feature as "Ducking". During ducking, volume control was disabled to avoid users from increasing the music volume and create echoes when their mic is on.

Past Implementation

Problem

We learned that users were often confused and frustrated when they were unable to adjust the volume while music was being ducked. This was because the disabeld state of the volume control was too subtle and there was no indication of when ducking was happening. They either didn’t know why their music was being lowered or why they couldn't adjust their volume.

In order to address these problems, we wanted to clarify how volume control works when music is being lowered, and simplify the volume control so it “just works”.

Newly Implemented Design

How Ducking Works

Remotion ducks music in two events: when someone in the room is talking, or when your own mic is unmuted. This was done so people can hear each other talk, and so your mic doesn’t pick up music coming from your speakers and create feedback for everyone else in the room.

Scenarios

The following were common behaviors that users wanted to take when music is being ducked:

1. User’s mic is on so the music is extremely low; the user wants to increase the music volume.
2. User’s mic is on and the user wants to mute the music volume.
3. Someone else is talking, music is still too loud or distracting and they want to either mute or lower the music volume.

In these scenarios, we wanted to avoid having users turn the music louder unless they muted themselves to prevent echo. We also wanted to let users know when music is ducking so they can take the right action based on their needs.

After assessing these common scenarios, we agreed as a team that users should be able to adjust their volume even if the music is ducking since it is a natural reaction for people to want to adjust the volume when their context changes.

Exploration

At the top-level, the goal was to find a way to simply communicate the concept of ducking so users can take an informed action — this was the impetus of the design exploration.

Design Explorations

An early concept was to include two volume sliders, one for the master volume and another for the ducking strength. Though I decided not to pursue this direction because it created more questions for users who don't fully understand the feature.

Another idea was to still automatically lower the volume slider but override the ducked volume when the user chooses to adjust the volume in any direction. The issue here was that we would be dismissing the concept of ducking when the user increases their music volume. We duck music to optimize for a better experience and this iteration defeated this purpose. Later on, this purpose helped me realize that the ducking state shouldn’t appear like an error or warning, thus I deviated from using yellow as an indicator.

Lastly, I wanted to introduce two toggles in a single slider — one for the ducked music volume and one for the voice volume. This UI enabled users to have control over both volumes while visually differentiating them. We didn't pursue this design due to the added complexity and it went against the philosophy of music in Remotion. It should “just work” so users don’t have to worry about the underlying complexity.

Final Design

Ultimately, I landed with a UI with an animated display of ducking to better reflect what is happening. Instead of showing a single volume slider moving back and forth, I proposed to visually separate the ducking volume by conveying that it is a portion of the master volume. The animation helped users understand what is happening by showing the progress from not-ducked to fully ducked.

The ducked volume is changed to a set percentage of the master volume, so when users adjust the master volume, the ducked volume will always be x% of the master volume. This way users don't have to worry about changing the ducking volume and simply control one volume slider.

Music Ducking Transition (Video)

We initially wanted to guide users to have the best experience by suggesting to turn off their mic if they wish to raise their music volume, but we chose to strip this out for simplicity sake. It required us to increase the size of the popover, which created an undesirable transition when going from not-ducked to fully ducked. Green was used as an indication to reflect the positive spirit of optimizing for a better experience.

Single Music Platform

Remotion Rooms are integrated with YouTube and Spotify to offer shared music in the background to cover the awkward silence when teams cowork online. YouTube plays by default when the first person joins the room, and can switch to Spotify if they have the Spotify Desktop App downloaded. Even though members can share their Spotify, others in the room can choose not to listen to the shared music and listen to YouTube instead.

Although this was the most popular feature amongst our customers, we learned that team members were still siloed and not sure what others in the room were experiencing. We realized that allowing members to listen to YouTube and Spotify simultaneously misaligned with the purpose behind Rooms — to encourage shared experiences for remote teams.

Past Implementation

Goal

With the problems that surfaced from allowing teams to listen to both music platforms at the same time, we saw the benefit of only allowing one music platform at a time to declutter the UI, remove the complexity that comes with enabling both platforms, and encourage teams to have a shared experience in Rooms.

To achieve these goals I aimed to make Spotify easy to discover, provide better context of the shared stream, and show who is listening to the shared Spotify stream.

Newly Implemented Design

Iteration

In one of the iterations, I focused on implicitly communicating the dynamic between the person sharing their music (DJ), and the people listening to the shared music (Listeners). I executed this by having the DJ accept a request when someone else wants to share Spotify or switch platforms. I believed this could be a way to establish roles within the room while giving each other a heads up when switching music streams.

Though I later realized that this was adding extra friction and complicated the experience. Plus, there wouldn’t be a DJ when YouTube is playing since it’s not a shared music stream. Thus I decided to keep the experience simple and lean in on the users to communicate with each other when switching streams.

Early Iteration: Request to Switch Music Platforms

Final Design

To align with Remotion’s plan to publicly launch the Spotify feature, we aimed to make Spotify easy to discover since YouTube is the default music player in Rooms. We wanted to guide the first person in the room to start sharing Spotify, and because we’ve shifted to only allow one music platform at a time, we wanted to encourage others to join in on the shared Spotify so they aren’t sitting in silence.

This was achieved by showing a popover when someone joins a Room for the first time as part of their onboarding experience. If a user doesn’t have the Spotify Desktop App or permissions enabled, they won’t be able to listen in. To support this case, we explained what is happening and made the popover actionable to resolve the issue.

First Time Joining a Room

When music is playing, I clarified who has control over the music depending on the context through copy refinement. I also replaced the “Change Song” button with the song thumbnail with the belief that people want to see song details. This UI update adds to the feeling of a shared experience and was complemented by exposing the listeners.

Listening to YouTube / Sharing Spotify

Lastly I had to find a way to notify people when the room is switching music platforms. Whenever the switch happens, members are notified with a popover that is actionable to opt-in or out right away.
