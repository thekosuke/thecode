Background

Seven is a health-focused startup building a system of products and services that make health mastery accessible to all through behavioral health coaching.

As their third designer, I led design for Seven’s digital offerings by owning the end-to-end experience of multiple projects while collaborating with cross-functional stakeholders. My main responsibilities were to revamp the client-facing mobile app while improving the internal web platform and the company website.

Reflection Activities

Reflection Activities are in-app activities that behavioral health coaches prescribe to help clients develop the right mindset to sustain healthy habits.

When I joined Seven, there was no digital component to this. The activities were simply HTML pages that gave clients instructions on what to do. There were 16 activities that had to be translated into an in-app experience so this required me to build a systemized design from scratch.

Before/After of Reflection Activities

Problem

The purpose of Reflection Activities was to help clients gain deeper self-awareness and develop self-efficacy through various content and activities provided by the coaches. In order to successfully deliver value through these activities, it was important to approach the problems from both, clients and coaches perspective.

The existing implementation was a simple HTML which didn’t provide a UI for users to interact with. This stagnant experience affected the credibility/trustability of the platform, which ultimately led to poor coaching outcomes. It also failed to contextualize the prompt and provide guidance for clients to get the most out of their activities.

Framing

In order to address these problems, I established four fundamental pillars that act as the DNA of all Reflection Activities:

1. Credibility of the activity, Getting buy-in from the clients
2. Managing expectations, Establishing the right mindset
3. Personalized value, Constructing meaning
4. Ongoing practice, Habit building

These pillars were then slotted into three different stages of the experience to identify the best point of the experience to inject value: Pre-Activity, In-Activity, and Post-Activity. Each pillar was framed with a question to inspire the explorations.

Proposed Framework

Systemized Design

With 16 different activities that follow a similar structure, breaking it down into three stages helped clarify how we might develop a reusable pattern across all activities. Each stage had its own UI pattern that enabled us to plug in content in a flexible manner. The short-term design system I created helped make the process for design and engineering more efficient in order to tackle the flows in parallel.

Each Reflection Activities adopted the same UI with different content

Wheel of Life

Among these reflection activities, Wheel of Life was a key component of Seven’s coaching—a tool for clients to assess different aspects of their life to identify a focus area to improve on. This activity was initially done on paper, so we translated this into a digital experience that clients can complete inside the app.

The product development process started with Product, Behavioral Health, and Design identifying the client and coach needs. Based on these discoveries, I explored different directions and pieced together elements that best aligned with our research findings.

Final Design of Wheel of Life

This process led to us narrow down the user experience into two potential paths. One was focused on practicality, and the other was an experimentation on dynamic interactions. We developed a research plan to conduct A/B testing for these two flows so we can validate the hypotheses we formed. 20 participants went through this study and we learned that there were different aspects of the two flows that resonated with people.

Planning for A/B Testing

The final design aims to guide clients through each area of life by zooming in and out of the wheel. This was done by starting with an overview of the wheel and allowing clients to tap into each area to set their score.

Many users neglected the description of each area, so I chose to have the score card fill up half of the screen upon appearance to present one item at a time. By doing so, the half-view afforded browsing different areas while the full-view enabled scoring.
