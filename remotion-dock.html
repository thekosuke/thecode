Background

Remotion is a virtual office that makes room for casual conversations with coworking and shared music. It is a macOS app that lets you pin your teammates to the dock to have background awareness of what people are up to.

I was brought in as a product designer in the Core Experience team to make focused improvements to the core app. The team consisted of 5 engineers and myself, with the goal to increase retention and word of mouth sharing.

Roadmap

The Core Experience Team's roadmap was driven by the most critical problems that led to user churn. Thus our goal was to hit the "Beta Bar" — a lower quality bar that we’re willing to accept as we’re in a period of rapid iteration. Even in this state, we need to provide an experience that’s good enough where a user likes our product enough to want to share it with others.

We focused on solving problems within the core workflows before investing in brand new features that drive new user value (which would be GA).

My Projects

The led design for two projects: Dock & Availability and Coworking with Music. Both workflows had critical problems that led to user churn and were not meeting the Beta Bar.

Primary Workflows of the Dock

The focus of the project was to address the problems with the main workflows that lead to user churn. We intentionally avoided re-imaginining the dock and app model overall, invest in brand new workflows and features that drive new user value, and convince users who are anti-dock to use our app. It was more about getting Remotion users who is okay with using the dock to love the dock.

Prioritized Problems

We learned that users have the dock minimized or hidden 59% of the time. This was because users had to choose between having a large dock with all their teammates or unpinning people. Users who don’t use the dock rely on under-developed surfaces like the mini dock, or overflow popovers.

Solution

The final design led to giving users more control, introducing dynamic contents in the dock, and clarifying status and availability. I also simplified the overall architecture by consolidating overlapping surfaces.

My Process

This is a peak into my design process. I explore numerous solutions on Figma while articulating my intent behind each decision. Writing helps me organize my thoughts while zooming in and out of the problem. By sharing the document with the team, it keeps the team aligned and understand where my head is at.

User Archetypes

As I continued to get feedback from teammates in different roles, I noticed a disconnect between what managers and ICs want in their dock. This made me question how the dock should be populated and how to prioritize items for different use cases. I asked what their current dock looks like and what their ideal dock would look like if we were to introduce the new model in order to consider different perspectives.

Dynamic Contents

Auto-populate contents in the dock based on the user’s favorites, teammate’s availability, and recent activity. Rethinking the pinning model so people don’t have to unpin teammates People were hesitant to unpin people because the UX around unpinned teammates were underdeveloped and was a hassle to access.

Give Users More Control

Enable users to customize the contents in their dock by giving fine tuned control over the number of certain items. What people want on their dock is very subjective to each individual so we wanted Remotion to allow people to customize their dock

Clarify Status & Availability

Simplify and clarify The most important information is whether someone is free to call or not

In response to the small dock getting busy, I stripped the dock to the essentials when set to Small. The most essential status is whether the person is available or not. Users can learn what others are up to via Tool Popover. By doing so, we reduce visual clutter and create breathing room for the avatars.
