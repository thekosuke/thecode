italiaDesign

Overview

In 2018, I was one of twelve senior students selected to participate in italiaDesign, an 8-month undergraduate field school program directed by Russell Taylor. The team interviewed emerging and established designers in Italy to learn how the discipline of design is practiced in a foreign context. We encapsulated these interviews in a web experience to contribute to the legacy of italiaDesign field school.

Challenge

Field school websites are launched annually with each team expressing their voice through the brand. Each team possess its own unique quality, though with that being said, the most recent team has a responsibility to respond to the previous years by introducing a new idea to the catalogue of field school websites. I believe this is one way to show our appreciation for being part of something greater than ourselves—to contribute to the legacy by sharing our learnings and applying it on this website.

With respect to the legacy, we aimed to deliver our own interpretation of the field school site by developing an identity that is unique to our experience in Italy. We challenged ourselves to introduce a new idea without breaking the existing architecture of the site.

Insight

Past field school sites each carried their own unique identity, though the annual launch of these sites have created a template-like pattern that lost a sense of purpose behind certain aspects of the site. We established a strategy with consideration to the cards we were dealt with in order to approach the project from a different angle.

Approach

The initial process was to establish a visual identity that would then be applied to the user experience. Though we recognized that this linear process overlooks the strategy behind the content organization, as each year contain different types of film content.

Instead, we followed a parallel process in which we strategically package the content to give substance to the user experience. We envisioned our content strategy to be the vehicle that enhance the brand. We were also able to design and build simultaneously, which allowed us to conduct user testing and validate our assumptions throughout the process.

We established design principles that would guide the process by acting as a filter to validate our decisions. We wanted the experience to reflect these values to communicate where we were coming from.

Ambiguity was an excerpt from The Vignelli Canon, in which spoke about how making contradictions in your design can enhance the quality of the work. We wondererd how this philosophy could be applied to a web experience.

While iterating on the landing page, we were unconsciously constraining ourselves to follow good UX practices, which could be a bad habit for UX designers. Not every experience has to be frictionless if we could sacrifice usability to increase impact. We recognized that this false constraint limited our opportunity to challenge people’s expectations.

It’s important to reduce frictions to achieve an intuitive user experience, but we also valued the emotional impact that could be evoked through memorable interactions. It was that 'Wow' moment we repeatedly encountered during user testing that made us wonder how we could push the boundaries even further. We wanted to bring excitement with purposeful interactions that enhance the greater narrative.

Execution

We created a landing page that encourage people to scroll and engage with the copy to get our message across upfront. This first impression created a sense of ambiguity that kept the audience curious enough to dig deeper.

By packaging the films into three distinct volumes, we implied a linear narrative that gave meaning to the order of content. With a hover interaction, we were able to showcase the captivating designer portraits complimented by the film title and volume description.

We wanted the films to be the first piece of content people engage with since it told the designers' story while allowing us to highlight and share some of our favorite moments.

We deviated from the conventional play button by utilizing the slitted type to suggest an interaction. This allowed us to reinforce our visual identity while giving it a functional purpose.

Because our films focused on the designers' unique practice, we saw value in including their work along with bite-size quotes that shared additional insights. This gave people an opportunity to see how these designers apply their philosophy onto their work, piece-by-piece.

At the end of each designer's story, we included a ‘Volume Preview’ which introduced the next designer with a quote that aligned with the theme of each Volume. The quote hinted the purpose behind each Volume while connecting the concept of the greater narrative.

“The reason for design is actually 'why design' Well there's lots of reasons why... how it can help and make things better, but I think the most core reason of why design is 'because'. Because I want it to be. So just kind of that point of conviction is where good things happen.”

— Sean Wolcott
